==============================================================================
  PART 6: SYNTHESIS — THE DIMENSIONAL BRIDGE
==============================================================================

  THE ARGUMENT:

  1. Open Eisenstein cells have boundary nodes with 3 neighbours.
     At dim=2, these nodes have undetectable single errors (d=1).
     Suppression scales polynomially: S ~ r.

  2. The merkabit doesn't live at dim=2. The dual-spinor architecture
     with pentachoric gates operates on S⁷×S⁷ (dim=4, quaternionic)
     or S¹⁵×S¹⁵ (dim=8, octonionic).

  3. The Hopf fibration at these dimensions creates INTERNAL
     detection channels: cross-coupling between Cayley-Dickson
     halves detects errors that spatial neighbours miss.

  4. These fiber channels operate in dimensions ORTHOGONAL to the
     2D lattice plane. They don't replace missing spatial neighbours
     — they provide detection from a DIFFERENT DIRECTION.

  5. The fiber detection doesn't depend on the node's position
     in the lattice (boundary vs interior). Every node has the
     same internal Hopf structure. This is the key: the fiber
     channels are UNIFORM, just like the torus.

  6. If the fiber channels catch enough of the boundary escapes,
     the effective code distance rises above 1, the boundary
     escape channel closes, and the Peierls argument applies.

  THE BRIDGE:
  ───────────
  Temporal periodicity (ouroboros cycle) — FAILED as bridge
    Detection saturates at τ=5; τ=12 adds nothing.

  Dimensional connectivity (Hopf fiber) — THIS TEST
    The fiber structure provides detection channels that
    operate in orthogonal dimensions to the spatial lattice.
    These channels are uniform across all nodes.
    They are the 'virtual neighbours' that close the boundary.

  The formal statement: the merkabit's native dimensionality
  (dim ≥ 4) provides Hopf fiber detection channels that
  compensate for missing spatial neighbours at boundary nodes,
  raising the effective code distance and enabling exponential
